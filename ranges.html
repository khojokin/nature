The Himalayas — Home of the Highest Peaks

The Himalayas are the highest mountain range in the world, stretching across several countries including Nepal, India, Bhutan, China, and Pakistan. This vast range contains many of Earth’s tallest mountains, including Mount Everest, and plays a major role in shaping weather patterns across Asia.

Formed by the collision of tectonic plates, the Himalayas continue to rise slowly over time. Their glaciers feed important river systems that provide water to millions of people.

Beyond their physical scale, the Himalayas are also rich in culture, spirituality, and biodiversity. Remote valleys, alpine forests, and snow-covered summits make them one of the most extraordinary natural regions in the world.

How Ranges Form

Most mountain ranges form when tectonic plates collide, fold, uplift, or trigger volcanic activity over millions of years.

Why They Matter

Mountain ranges influence rainfall, store freshwater in glaciers, and support unique plants, animals, and human communities.

Global Importance

From climate regulation to tourism and biodiversity, mountain systems play a major role in life across the planet.
