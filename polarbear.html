Polar Bear

The polar bear is the largest bear in the world and one of the Arctic’s most iconic predators. Built for life on sea ice, it swims powerfully, hunts seals, and depends on frozen marine habitats for traveling, resting, mating, and in some regions denning.

Physical Profile

Polar bears are the largest bears in the world. Adult males commonly weigh around 800 to 1,300 pounds, while females are usually lighter, around 300 to 700 pounds. Body length is often about 6 to 9 feet.

In the wild, polar bears commonly live about 25 to 30 years. Captive individuals can sometimes live longer.

Distinguishing features include creamy white fur, black skin beneath the fur, a long neck, narrow head, powerful limbs, large paws, and a heavily insulated body with thick fat reserves.

Anatomical adaptations include:

  • large paws that work like snowshoes and swimming paddles
  • dense fur and thick blubber for insulation
  • powerful forelimbs for swimming long distances
  • sharp claws and teeth for catching seals
  • excellent sense of smell for detecting prey under snow or across long distances

Sexual dimorphism is strong. Males are much larger and heavier than females.

Habitat & Geography

Polar bears live across the Arctic and depend heavily on sea ice. Their range includes the Arctic Ocean, sea ice, and adjacent coastal zones in places such as Alaska, Canada, Greenland, Norway, and Russia.

Their primary biome is Arctic marine ice habitat. Unlike forest bears, polar bears are deeply tied to frozen ocean surfaces because that is where they travel, hunt, and in some areas reach denning grounds.

Territory size varies enormously depending on season, ice conditions, prey movement, and sex. Maternal dens are made in snowdrifts or snow-covered land areas by pregnant females.

Diet & Hunting

Polar bears are apex predators and highly carnivorous. Their main prey are ice-associated seals, especially ringed seals and bearded seals.

Their hunting strategy often involves waiting quietly at seal breathing holes, stalking seals on ice, or using sea ice as a platform to access marine prey. Because of this, sea ice is not just part of the landscape — it is the foundation of their hunting ecology.

Water requirements are also tied to the Arctic environment, where they gain moisture from prey and the conditions around them.

Behavior & Social Structure

Polar bears are mostly solitary. The main exceptions are mothers with cubs and temporary pairing during breeding. They may also come together around food sources, but they do not form permanent social groups like herd mammals.

Communication includes scent marking, body posture, vocal sounds, and close-range interactions. Their movement patterns and seasonal behavior are strongly influenced by the presence or absence of sea ice.

Polar bears do not follow a strict simple diurnal/nocturnal schedule in the way some animals do. Activity changes with season, light conditions, prey availability, and ice conditions.

Mating Rituals & Reproduction

Polar bears breed seasonally. Pregnant females enter winter dens and give birth there. Cubs are born very small, blind, and weak compared with the adult body size.

A typical litter is usually one or two cubs, though twins are especially well known. Mothers provide intense parental care, and cubs may remain with their mother for up to about two and a half years.

Evolution & History

Polar bears are part of the bear family Ursidae and are closely related to brown bears. Their evolutionary history reflects adaptation to Arctic marine conditions, especially ice-based hunting and extreme cold.

Over time, they evolved traits suited to sea ice living, including body insulation, marine hunting specialization, and long-distance swimming ability.

Conservation & Threats

The biggest threat to polar bears is climate change-driven sea ice loss. As sea ice melts earlier and forms later, bears lose access to the platforms they rely on for hunting, resting, traveling, mating, and in some places denning.

Other threats include pollution, industrial disturbance, shipping, conflict with humans in Arctic communities, and potential risks from oil and gas activity.

Current global population estimates are around 26,000 individuals, though different subpopulations are faring differently. Some appear stable, some may be increasing, and some are declining.

Cultural & Human Impact

Polar bears have major significance in Arctic Indigenous cultures, northern identity, conservation storytelling, and global climate awareness. They are one of the strongest wildlife symbols of the Arctic.

Economically and socially, they influence tourism, conservation work, community safety planning, and international climate discussion.
