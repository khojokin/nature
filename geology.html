How Mountains Are Formed

Mountains form through powerful geological forces deep within the Earth's crust. Most mountains are created when tectonic plates collide, forcing the Earth's crust to fold and rise.

Over millions of years these slow movements build enormous mountain ranges such as the Himalayas and the Andes.

Volcanic activity can also form mountains when magma rises through the crust and solidifies into towering volcanic peaks.

Erosion from wind, water, and glaciers constantly reshapes mountains, carving valleys, cliffs, and dramatic landscapes.

Types of Mountains

Fold Mountains

Formed when tectonic plates collide and push layers of rock upward. Examples include the Himalayas and the Alps.

Volcanic Mountains

Created when magma erupts and solidifies. Famous examples include Mount Fuji and Mount St. Helens.

Block Mountains

Formed when large sections of Earth's crust are uplifted along faults due to tectonic forces.

Dome Mountains

Created when magma pushes the Earth's crust upward but does not erupt onto the surface.

Tectonic Plates

The Earth's crust is divided into plates that slowly move and collide, forming mountains.

Erosion

Wind, water, and glaciers gradually wear down mountains over millions of years.

Geological Time

Mountain formation happens extremely slowly, often taking tens of millions of years.
